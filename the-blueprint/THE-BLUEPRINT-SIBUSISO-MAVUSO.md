## Before anything else...

Look, I know this is unconventional. A whole document? About me? But hear me out.

I believe in intentionality. I believe that when two people decide to walk together, they should actually know where they're walking to. So instead of discovering things about me piece by piece over months or years, I figured... why not just put it all out there?

This is me. Unfiltered. The good stuff, the hard stuff, and everything in between.

---

## Where I come from

My name is Sbue, AKA Sibusiso Mavuso. Most call me Bafo.

I was born on October 21, 1994, in Eswatini - a small kingdom that most people confuse with Switzerland. My home is in Mankayane, a place called Lushikishini. My parents are Lucky Godfrey Mavuso and Olivia Tobhatane Shabangu.

Here is something random about me - I was born with a full set of teeth. Yes, actual teeth. Apparently that is a thing that happens sometimes. I do not know what it means, but it is mine. It is part of my story.

I am the firstborn. My dad was also the firstborn of six. So by default, I became the one everyone looks to. The deputy parent at home. The older brother to all my cousins. It is not a role I chose, but it is one I have grown into.

---

## The day everything changed

May 5, 2001.

I was six years old. The day before, on May 4th, my father sat me down and told me he was leaving. I didn't understand what he meant. I was a child. How could I?

But he said something that I have never forgotten:

*"Take good care of your mother and your sister."*

I said yes. I did not know what I was agreeing to.

That was the last time I saw him.

Those words have followed me everywhere. Every business I've started, every decision I've made... if I'm being honest, it all comes back to that moment. A promise I made when I was too young to understand what promises really meant.

---

## The hustle started early

By the time I was 13, I was already making money.

I was in boarding school - Form 1, Grade 8. Most kids were asking their parents for pocket money. Me? When my mom came to visit, I was the one giving her money. Not the other way around.

I am not saying this to brag. I am saying it because that is what my father's words did to me. They woke something up. I understood early that waiting for life to happen to you is not an option when people are depending on you.

At 17, I started my first real business - bean farming.

Why beans? Honestly, because I love beans. They genuinely make me happy. But then I discovered that beans are in short supply across the SADC region, and suddenly my lunch preference became a business opportunity. That is kind of how things have worked out for me - passion and opportunity keep meeting in strange places.

---

## The university years

In 2012, I left Eswatini and came to South Africa to study Accounting Science at the University of Pretoria. I was 17.

Four majors. Auditing was my favorite.

But here is the thing nobody likes to admit - I failed accounting in my third year. The irony, right? An accounting student failing accounting.

But that failure taught me something. If I struggled with this, there must be thousands of others struggling too. So when I graduated in 2018, I started Sbue Golden Business Solutions. My logic was simple: three years of studying means I know something. Let me help people who are where I was.

My graduation day was September 8, 2018. The same day my grandfather passed away.

His last words, they told me, were: *"Now you will be able to take care of my daughter."*

My daughter. He meant my mom.

Another man I loved. Another charge passed to me.

---

## Celebrating while we can

In 2019, I did something that meant a lot to me.

I celebrated my grandparents. While they were still alive.

I called everyone - my family, their friends - bought a cow, had it slaughtered. It was my way of saying thank you before it was too late.

My grandfather passed in March 2020. But he left knowing he was celebrated. That matters to me more than I can put into words.

## The vision

This is the part that might sound crazy. But it's real to me.

I've spent a lot of time thinking about Africa. About why we struggle. About what happened to us - not just the physical chains of slavery, but the mental chains that still exist.

There's a system that tells us we're not enough. That pushes narratives of self-hate. That makes us consumers instead of creators. That makes us wait to be fed instead of feeding ourselves.

I want to change that.

Not through speeches. Through systems. Through businesses that prove we can. Through solutions built by Africans, for Africans.

I want to be a voice that advises nations. I want to influence how money moves across this continent. I want my work to matter long after I am gone.

*We found our communities poor. I am not leaving them poor.*

## On love and relationships

This part matters. So let me be clear about where I stand.

I follow what I call the Kingdom way. Throughout Scripture, there's this principle that I've held onto:

*Love who you marry. Do not marry who you love.*

Now, this sounds backwards. But hear me out.

Most people do it the other way. They have a checklist. They look for someone who ticks the boxes. They fall in love with what they see, and then they decide to marry. The problem? You are making a decision based on a snapshot. You are marrying the version of someone you have seen so far.

What happens when the other parts show up? The parts you did not see during the honeymoon phase? The weird habits? The fears? The flaws? Now you are stuck with someone who does not match the picture you fell in love with.

I believe in the opposite.

I believe in committing first, and then loving what unfolds. I will get to know you on the way. Every day, something new will come out - a fear, a dream, a quirk, something you have been hiding - and I learn to love that too. Not because I chose it from a menu, but because it is part of you.

This way, there are no surprises that break us. Just discoveries that deepen us.

It is not about finding someone perfect. It is about choosing someone and then loving every imperfect thing you discover along the journey.

## How I see partnership

In Scripture, woman is called "Ezer." It's the same word God uses to describe Himself as our helper. Let that sit for a moment.

A woman is not an afterthought. She is not secondary. She is essential.

The man's role, like a lion, is to create an environment where the woman can flourish. In Proverbs 31, the man sits at the gates with the elders - not because he is lazy, but because he is guarding what comes in and what stays out. He is creating safety. She might be more capable of nourishing the vision than he is - and that is beautiful. She just needs to know she is safe.

If you can do something better than me, do it. I will be right here making sure you feel safe enough to try.
