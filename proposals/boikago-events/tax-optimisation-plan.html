Section 01

Background & Objective

Understanding the client's needs and the intended tax-efficient outcome

1.1 Client Overview

Boikago Events & Creatives (Pty) Ltd ("the Company") is a registered company in which the director/shareholder ("the Director") is also an employee of the Company. The Director has dependent children for whom school fees are payable.

1.2 Objective

The Director wishes to have the Company pay the school fees of her dependent children as part of a tax-efficient remuneration structure. The objective of this plan is to:

  1. Maximise the tax benefit

    Structure the payment of school fees to achieve the greatest possible reduction in overall tax liability — both at company and personal level.

  2. Ensure full SARS compliance

    Ensure the arrangement is fully compliant with the Income Tax Act 58 of 1962, the Seventh Schedule, and the Tax Administration Act 28 of 2011.

  3. Create an audit-defensible structure

    Produce the necessary documentation, board resolutions, and supporting evidence to withstand any SARS audit or objection.

  4. Optimise travel allowance

    Structure a compliant travel allowance to further reduce personal tax liability through legitimate deductions.

⚠️ Important Disclaimer

This plan is structured to achieve a legitimate tax benefit through mechanisms expressly provided for in the Income Tax Act. This is tax avoidance (legal) — not tax evasion (illegal). The arrangement has genuine economic substance and commercial rationale beyond the tax benefit, as supported by the case law cited herein.

2.2 Key Provision — Bursary Exemption

📜 Seventh Schedule — Paragraph 12B

"The cash equivalent of the value of a taxable benefit contemplated in paragraph 2(h) shall be deemed to be nil where —

(a) the benefit is a bona fide bursary or scholarship granted by the employer to an employee or a relative of an employee to study at a recognised educational or research institution…

(b) in the case of a qualification to which NQF levels 1 to 4 apply (including Grade R to Grade 12), the remuneration proxy of the employee during that year of assessment does not exceed R600,000."

This is the cornerstone provision. Where the Director's total remuneration (remuneration proxy) is structured to be at or below R600,000 per annum, any bursary granted by the Company for the schooling of the Director's children at NQF Level 1–4 (Grade R to Grade 12) is entirely exempt from fringe benefit tax.

2.3 Key Provision — Deductibility for the Company

📜 Income Tax Act — Section 11(a)

"For the purpose of determining the taxable income derived by any person from carrying on any trade, there shall be allowed as deductions from the income of such person so derived… expenditure and losses actually incurred in the production of the income, provided such expenditure and losses are not of a capital nature."

The bursary paid by the Company is part of the Director's remuneration package. As remuneration is incurred in the production of income (the Director renders services to the Company), the entire bursary amount is a deductible expense for the Company under Section 11(a).

2.4 General Anti-Avoidance Rules (GAAR)

📜 Income Tax Act — Section 80A

"An avoidance arrangement is an impermissible avoidance arrangement if its sole or main purpose was to obtain a tax benefit and — (a) in the context of business, it was entered into or carried out by means or in a manner which would not normally be employed for bona fide business purposes… or (b) it lacks commercial substance…"

Our arrangement withstands GAAR scrutiny because:

  • The bursary scheme is expressly provided for in the Seventh Schedule — it is a mechanism Parliament intended employers to use
  • It has commercial substance — it forms part of the Director's total cost to company remuneration
  • It is not abnormal — thousands of South African employers offer bursary schemes as part of remuneration packages
  • There is no misuse or abuse of the provision — we comply with every condition stipulated in paragraph 12B

3.3 Remuneration Proxy Structuring

The "remuneration proxy" is defined in paragraph 1 of the Seventh Schedule as the total of all amounts of remuneration (as defined in the Fourth Schedule) received by or accrued to the employee during the year of assessment, excluding the bursary itself and certain retirement fund contributions.

To qualify for the exemption, the Director's cash salary + other taxable benefits must not exceed R600,000 per annum. This requires careful structuring of the total remuneration package.

Example Structure:

Component Annual Amount Tax Treatment
Cash salary R 480,000 Taxable (within R600k proxy)
Retirement fund contribution (employer) R 60,000 Excluded from proxy
Medical aid contribution (employer) R 60,000 Included in proxy
Remuneration proxy R 540,000 Below R600k ✓
Bursary — Child 1 school fees R 80,000 Tax-Free
Bursary — Child 2 school fees R 80,000 Tax-Free
Total Cost to Company R 760,000
💰 Tax Saving — Bursary Route

The R160,000 in school fees is exempt from income tax in the Director's hands (paragraph 12B). Without this structure, paying R160,000 in school fees from personal after-tax income (at a marginal rate of, say, 31%) would cost approximately R231,884 in pre-tax salary. The saving is approximately R71,884 per year in personal tax alone.

Additionally, the full R160,000 remains a deductible expense for the Company, reducing its taxable income and saving a further R43,200 in company tax (at 27%).

3.4 What the Bursary May Cover

Per SARS Interpretation Note 14 and paragraph 12B, the bursary may cover:

  • Tuition fees
  • Registration and examination fees
  • Books and study materials
  • School uniforms (if required by the school)
  • School-related equipment (as prescribed by the institution)
⚠️ What It May NOT Cover

The bursary exemption does not extend to transport costs, meals, accommodation (unless boarding is a condition of the bursary), or extracurricular activities that are not part of the formal curriculum. These must be paid separately and will not qualify for exemption.

Section 04

School Fees — Salary Sacrifice Alternative

A fallback if the remuneration proxy exceeds R600,000

If the Director's remuneration proxy exceeds R600,000 (e.g., because the business requires a higher salary), the bursary exemption under paragraph 12B will not apply for NQF Level 1–4 education. In this scenario, the school fees become a taxable fringe benefit under paragraph 2(e) or 2(h) of the Seventh Schedule.

4.1 How It Works

The Company still pays the school fees directly, but the amount is included on the Director's IRP5 as a fringe benefit. The Director pays income tax on the fringe benefit value.

Still Beneficial Because:

  • The Company still gets a Section 11(a) deduction for the school fees as remuneration expense
  • The school fees are paid from pre-tax company money — administratively simpler than salary → personal account → school
  • Cash flow is optimised — the Director doesn't need to receive cash, pay personal tax, then pay school fees from what remains
📜 Seventh Schedule — Paragraph 2(e)

"A taxable benefit shall be deemed to have been granted by an employer to an employee if… any service has been rendered… to the employee or to a dependant or other relative of such employee, by the employer at the cost of the employer."

Section 06

Travel Allowance — Structure & Requirements

Understanding the mechanics, compliance requirements, and tax benefits of a travel allowance

6.1 What Is a Travel Allowance?

A travel allowance is a fixed amount paid by an employer to an employee to compensate for the use of the employee's private motor vehicle for business travel. It is governed by Section 8(1)(b) of the Income Tax Act read with the SARS Fixing of Rate per Kilometre published annually in the Government Gazette.

📜 Income Tax Act — Section 8(1)(b)

"Where any allowance or advance referred to in section 8(1)(b)(i)(A) has been received by or accrued to a holder of an office or an employee during any year of assessment, there shall be included in the income of that taxpayer an amount equal to such allowance or advance, less whatever deduction the taxpayer is entitled to under section 11(e) or paragraph 7 of the Seventh Schedule…"

6.2 How It Works — Step by Step

  1. Company pays a fixed monthly travel allowance

    As part of the Director's employment contract, a fixed travel allowance is included (e.g., R6,000 per month = R72,000 per annum).

  2. 80% is included in taxable income for PAYE

    During the year, 80% of the travel allowance is subject to PAYE. If the employee can demonstrate that the vehicle is used more than 80% for business, only 20% is subject to PAYE.

  3. Director keeps a SARS-compliant logbook

    The Director records EVERY trip — business and private — in a logbook showing date, destination, purpose, and distance (km). This is legally required to claim the deduction.

  4. At year-end, claim the business portion

    On the annual tax return (ITR12), the Director claims the business travel deduction. The difference between the allowance received and the deductible amount determines the net taxable travel income.

6.3 Two Methods of Claiming

Method 1: SARS Deemed Cost Table (Simplified)

Use the SARS prescribed rate table based on the value of the vehicle. For the 2025/2026 tax year:

Vehicle Value (incl. VAT) Fixed Cost p.a. Fuel Cost per km Maintenance per km
R 0 – R 107,133 R 30,597 R 1.53 R 0.45
R 107,134 – R 214,522 R 54,582 R 1.53 R 0.58
R 214,523 – R 321,911 R 73,867 R 1.57 R 0.64
R 321,912 – R 429,300 R 93,267 R 1.68 R 0.77
R 429,301 – R 536,688 R 104,440 R 1.73 R 0.88
R 536,689 – R 644,077 R 116,062 R 1.82 R 0.97
R 644,078 and above R 116,062 R 1.82 R 0.97

Note: The SARS table caps the vehicle value at R 644,077 for deemed cost purposes. Vehicles above this value use the same rate as the maximum bracket.

Method 2: Actual Cost Method

The Director may alternatively claim actual vehicle costs (fuel, insurance, maintenance, finance charges, depreciation) multiplied by the business-use percentage as determined by the logbook. This method often yields a higher deduction for high-mileage business users.

6.4 The Logbook — Absolute Requirement

⚠️ Non-Negotiable — SARS Requirement

Without a logbook, no deduction is allowed. SARS will disallow the entire travel deduction if a valid logbook is not maintained. The logbook must run for the full tax year (1 March to 28/29 February) and must record:

Date Date of each trip 15 March 2026

📜 SARS Interpretation Note 14 (Issue 5)

"Employees who receive a travel allowance must maintain a logbook of business kilometres travelled during the year of assessment. The opening and closing odometer readings for each year of assessment must be recorded. In the absence of a logbook, no deduction under section 8(1)(b) will be allowed."

6.5 Example Calculation

Assuming: Travel allowance of R72,000/year, vehicle value R350,000, total km 25,000, business km 15,000 (60%):

Item Amount
Travel allowance received R 72,000
Deemed fixed cost (R93,267 × 60%) R 55,960
Deemed fuel cost (15,000 × R1.68) R 25,200
Deemed maintenance (15,000 × R0.77) R 11,550
Total deduction R 92,710
Net taxable travel income R 0 (deduction exceeds allowance)
💰 Travel Allowance Benefit

In this example, the entire R72,000 travel allowance is effectively tax-free because the deemed cost deduction (R92,710) exceeds the allowance received. The excess deduction of R20,710 can even offset other taxable income. The Director receives R72,000 extra per year and pays zero additional tax on it.

Section 07

Case Law & Legal Precedent

South African court decisions supporting the legitimacy of this tax structure
⚖️ Case Law
Commissioner for SARS v NWK Ltd [2011] ZASCA 168; 2011 (2) SA 67 (SCA)

Principle: The Supreme Court of Appeal confirmed that courts will look at the substance of a transaction, not merely its form. However, where a transaction has genuine commercial substance and is not a sham, the taxpayer is entitled to arrange their affairs to minimise tax.

Application to us: Our bursary scheme is not a sham — the Director is a genuine employee, the children genuinely attend school, the fees are genuinely paid. The arrangement has commercial substance (employee retention and remuneration).

⚖️ Case Law
IRC v Duke of Westminster [1936] AC 1 (House of Lords, applied in SA)

Principle: "Every man is entitled, if he can, to order his affairs so that the tax attaching under the appropriate Acts is less than it otherwise would be." This foundational principle of legitimate tax planning has been repeatedly endorsed by South African courts.

Application to us: The Director is exercising her right to structure her remuneration in the most tax-efficient manner allowed by law.

⚖️ Case Law
Ovenstone v SIR [1980] (2) SA 721 (A)

Principle: Expenditure is deductible under Section 11(a) if it is incurred "in the production of income" and is not of a capital nature. The court held that there must be a sufficiently close nexus between the expenditure and the income-earning activities.

Application to us: The bursary is part of the Director's remuneration package. Remuneration paid to employees who produce the company's income is clearly deductible.

⚖️ Case Law
CSARS v Brummeria Renaissance (Pty) Ltd [2007] ZASCA 95; 69 SATC 205

Principle: The SCA confirmed that benefits received in a form other than cash (such as the right to use interest-free loans) still constitute "gross income" and must be valued and included. This reinforces that the Seventh Schedule is the correct mechanism for valuing non-cash employment benefits.

Application to us: We are correctly declaring the bursary under the Seventh Schedule framework, and where paragraph 12B exempts it, SARS has no basis to include it again.

⚖️ Case Law
ITC 1735 (2001) 64 SATC 455

Principle: The Tax Court dealt with the distinction between benefits provided to employees as part of remuneration versus personal gifts. Where an employer's policy formalises a benefit, and the employee renders services in exchange, the benefit is employment-related and must be dealt with under the appropriate provisions of the ITA.

Application to us: Our formal bursary policy ensures the benefit is treated as employment-related (not a donation), making it subject to the favourable treatment under paragraph 12B.

⚖️ Case Law
SBI v Smit [1955] (1) SA 202 (A)

Principle: The court held that in determining whether expenditure was incurred "in the production of income," one must consider the purpose of the expenditure and whether it was closely connected to the taxpayer's income-earning operations.

Application to us: The purpose of the bursary expenditure is remuneration — it is directly connected to the employment relationship and the Company's trade.

Section 08

Implementation Roadmap

Step-by-step guide to implementing the tax optimisation plan
  1. Confirm the Director's Employment Status

    Ensure the Director has a valid written employment contract with Boikago Events & Creatives (Pty) Ltd. If one does not exist, we will draft it. This is the foundational requirement — without it, no employee benefits can be structured.

    Week 1

  2. Draft & Adopt the Bursary Policy

    SGBS Group will prepare a formal company bursary/scholarship policy. This policy must be adopted by board resolution. It must apply to all qualifying employees (even if the Director is the only current employee — the policy must be open to others).

    Week 1

  3. Pass a Board Resolution

    The board of directors must resolve to: (a) adopt the bursary policy, (b) grant a bursary to the Director's children for the current tax year, and (c) approve the total bursary amount. Minutes must be formally recorded and signed.

    Week 1

  4. Restructure the Remuneration Package

    Adjust the Director's total CTC to ensure the remuneration proxy remains at or below R600,000. SGBS will prepare a formal remuneration restructuring letter for the employment file.

    Week 2

  5. Set Up Direct Payment to the School

    The Company pays the school fees directly from the company bank account. This creates a clear paper trail. Do not reimburse the Director for fees already paid from personal funds — the company must pay the school directly.

    Week 2

  6. Update Payroll / IRP5 Processing

    The bursary must be reflected on the EMP501 reconciliation and the Director's IRP5 using the correct SARS source code: Code 3815 (bursaries and scholarships). If exempt under para 12B, it is reported but not included in taxable income.

    Week 2

  7. Implement Travel Allowance

    Add a fixed travel allowance to the employment contract. Issue the Director a logbook (or set up a digital logbook app). Begin recording all trips from Day 1 of the tax year. Reflect the travel allowance on the IRP5 using Code 3701.

    Week 2–3

  8. File and Archive All Documentation

    Create a dedicated tax file containing all supporting documents (listed in Section 9 below). This file is your audit defence pack — it must be maintained and updated every year.

    Ongoing

Section 10

Risk Assessment & SARS Audit Defence

Identifying potential risks and our defence strategy

10.1 Potential SARS Challenges

Potential Challenge Risk Level Our Defence
"The bursary scheme is not bona fide" Low We have a formal written policy, board resolution, and the scheme is open to all employees.
"The remuneration proxy exceeds R600,000" Low We have documented the CTC restructuring. Payslips and IRP5 confirm the proxy is within limit.
"GAAR applies — sole purpose is tax avoidance" Low The bursary scheme is expressly provided for in the Seventh Schedule. It has commercial substance (employee remuneration). Per Duke of Westminster, taxpayers may arrange affairs to minimise tax.
"The Director is not a genuine employee" Low Employment contract, UIF contributions, PAYE deductions, and daily involvement in business operations all evidence genuine employment.
"Travel logbook is inadequate" Medium This is the most common area of challenge. Mitigation: use a digital logbook app, record trips daily, never back-date entries.

10.2 Penalties for Non-Compliance

⚠️ Tax Administration Act — Penalties

Section 222: An understatement penalty of between 10% and 200% of the tax shortfall may be imposed, depending on the taxpayer's behaviour (from "substantial understatement" to "intentional tax evasion").

Section 234: Criminal offences for wilfully and without just cause failing to furnish documents, filing false returns, or making false statements. Penalties include fines and up to 2 years imprisonment.

Our arrangement carries minimal risk because: (a) we are disclosing everything on the IRP5 and tax returns, (b) we are relying on provisions expressly available in the Act, and (c) we have documented professional advice (this plan).

10.3 Defence Strategy Summary

🛡️ Our Three-Line Defence

Line 1 — Legislative Authority: Paragraph 12B of the Seventh Schedule expressly provides for the bursary exemption. We meet every condition. SARS cannot override what Parliament has enacted.

Line 2 — Commercial Substance: The arrangement is part of a genuine employment relationship with a bona fide remuneration structure. It is not a sham (NWK test passed).

Line 3 — Full Disclosure: Every element is declared on the IRP5, EMP501, and income tax returns. There is no concealment. Section 222 penalties require understatement — we have stated everything.
