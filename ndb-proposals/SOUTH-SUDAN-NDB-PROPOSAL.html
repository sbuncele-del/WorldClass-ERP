Executive Summary

This business plan presents the establishment of the National Development Bank of South Sudan (NDB-SS), a specialized development finance institution designed to mobilize investment capital for strategic national infrastructure and economic development projects.

Modeled after successful development finance institutions such as the Development Bank of Southern Africa (DBSA), African Development Bank (AfDB), and Qatar Development Bank, NDB-SS will serve as the primary vehicle for channeling both commercial investment and donor funding into transformative projects across South Sudan and the broader African region.

Seed Capital Request

We are seeking USD 10,000,000 (Ten Million US Dollars) in seed capital to establish and operationalize the National Development Bank of South Sudan. This seed investment will fund:

  • Legal establishment and regulatory licensing
  • Initial operations and working capital (12 months)
  • Technology infrastructure and banking systems
  • Senior management team recruitment
  • First project pipeline development

This seed capital will enable NDB-SS to mobilize USD 2 Billion in Year 1 through deal origination, earning approximately USD 100 Million in commission revenue.

2. Market Opportunity

2.1 South Sudan Development Context

South Sudan, the world's youngest nation (independence 2011), faces enormous development challenges but also unprecedented opportunities for transformative investment.

Key Economic Indicators:

Indicator Value
Population ~12 million
GDP ~$4.5 billion
GDP per Capita ~$375
Economic Dependence 95%+ on oil exports
Infrastructure Gap $50+ billion estimated
Road Network <200km paved (entire country)
Electrification Rate <5%
Banking Penetration <10%

2.2 Infrastructure Deficit

Roads & Transport:

  • Less than 200km of paved roads in a country larger than France
  • No functioning railway network
  • Limited air transport infrastructure
  • River transport underutilized despite extensive Nile system

Energy:

  • Less than 5% of population has electricity access
  • Massive hydropower potential (Nile) untapped
  • Solar potential among highest in Africa

Agriculture:

  • Only 4% of arable land currently cultivated
  • Massive irrigation potential from Nile basin
  • Food imports despite agricultural potential

2.3 Funding Landscape

Current Development Finance Sources:

Gap Analysis:

  • Required Annual Investment: $5-10 billion for meaningful development
  • Current Investment: <$3 billion (mostly humanitarian)
  • Funding Gap: $2-7 billion annually
  • Development Finance Gap: No dedicated DFI to structure bankable projects

2.4 Regional Opportunity

East African Community Integration:

  • South Sudan seeking EAC membership
  • Regional infrastructure connectivity needed
  • Cross-border trade potential with Uganda, Kenya, Ethiopia, DRC

Pan-African Development Agenda:

  • Programme for Infrastructure Development in Africa (PIDA)
  • Agenda 2063

3. Business Model

3.1 Dual-Track Approach

NDB-SS operates two complementary business streams:

Track A: Commercial Development Finance

  • Source investment funds for viable, revenue-generating projects
  • Structure deals to ensure repayment
  • Focus: Infrastructure with commercial returns (toll roads, power, mining)

Track B: Donor Fund Coordination

  • Attract and manage donor/humanitarian funding
  • Channel to development-aligned humanitarian projects
  • Focus: Social infrastructure (schools, hospitals, water systems)

3.2 Revenue Model

Primary Revenue Streams:

Revenue Source Description Rate/Fee
Deal Origination Commission Fee for connecting funders to bankable projects 5% of deal value

Transaction/Paymaster Fee Fee for disbursement management 0.5-1% of disbursements
Advisory Services Project preparation, feasibility studies Project-based

8. Risk Analysis & Mitigation

8.1 Key Risks

Risk Category Risk Likelihood Impact Mitigation
Political Instability, conflict High High Diversify geography, donor partnerships
Economic Oil price volatility High Medium Focus on non-oil sectors
Credit Project defaults Medium High Rigorous due diligence, guarantees
Operational Capacity constraints Medium Medium International partnerships, training
Regulatory Policy changes Low Medium Government engagement, charter protection
Reputational Project failures Medium High Strong governance, impact monitoring
FX Currency volatility High Medium USD-denominated operations

8.2 Risk Mitigation Framework

Political Risk Mitigation:

  • Multilateral co-investment (MIGA, AfDB guarantees)
  • Political risk insurance
  • Diversification across regions (East Africa, Pan-African)
  • Strong government relations with independence

Credit Risk Mitigation:

  • Comprehensive due diligence
  • Government guarantees where possible
  • Collateral and security packages
  • Phased disbursements tied to milestones

Operational Risk Mitigation:

  • International advisory partnerships
  • Robust internal controls
  • Technology infrastructure
  • Staff development programs
